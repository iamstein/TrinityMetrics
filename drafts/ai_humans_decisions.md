# AI Can Assist. Humans Must Own the Decision.

Generative AI is rapidly becoming capable of writing code, fitting models, and producing analyses in minutes that only a year ago would have taken days. This creates clear productivity gains.

But in drug development, analytical outputs are not just information—they are **claims with consequences**. Those claims ultimately support decisions that must be owned.

- A dose selection affects patient safety.
- A model interpretation shapes clinical strategy.
- A clinical trial directs hundreds of millions of dollars and years of work.

These are not outputs to be generated and passed along; they are decisions that someone must be willing to own.

> The person making a claim must be exposed to what happens if that claim is wrong—scientifically, professionally, and ethically. That exposure is what drives rigor.

AI systems cannot own decisions in this way; they generate outputs without stake. They optimize for plausibility and coherence, not for correctness under consequence. But there is risk that well-formed AI outputs can carry authority without corresponding responsibility.

The primary risk of our increasing reliance on AI is that as analyses become faster and more polished:

- reasoning becomes less visible
- outputs feel more complete than they are
- human review becomes more superficial

Over time, authority may shift from **owned judgment** to **generated output**. This looks like efficiency, but in a regulated setting, it is unacceptable. The goal then is to integrate AI without transferring authority.

> **AI may assist in generating and analyzing claims, but humans must explicitly own any claim that informs a decision.**

This has practical implications.

- **Analysis and decision must remain distinct.** AI can accelerate modeling and exploration, but concluding—turning results into a decision—must remain human.

- **Reasoning must be visible.** Conclusions should not be buried in reports. It should be clear what is being claimed, what supports it, and what could be wrong.

- **Completeness is not correctness.** As outputs look more finished, the burden on the reviewer increases, not decreases.
